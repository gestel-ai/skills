# Marketing Psychology & Mental Models Catalog

A working library of psychological principles and mental models for marketing
decisions. Each entry gives the principle and a concrete marketing application.
Use it to diagnose why people do or don't act, then to design ethical
interventions. Apply only models that fit the user's actual situation; do not
dump the whole list.

---

## 1. Foundational Thinking Models

Sharpen strategy and make sure you are solving the right problem.

- **First Principles** — Break problems to basic truths and rebuild. Use the 5
  Whys instead of copying competitors. *Don't assume you need content marketing
  because competitors do; ask what problem it actually solves.*
- **Jobs to Be Done** — People "hire" products to get a job done; focus on the
  outcome, not features. *A drill buyer wants a hole, not a drill.*
- **Circle of Competence** — Stay where you have genuine expertise; venture out
  only with learning or expert help. *Don't chase every channel.*
- **Inversion** — Ask "what would guarantee failure?" then avoid it. *List what
  would make the campaign fail (confusing message, wrong audience, slow page)
  and prevent each.*
- **Occam's Razor** — Simplest explanation usually wins. *If conversions drop,
  check broken form / page speed before complex attribution theories.*
- **Pareto Principle (80/20)** — ~80% of results come from ~20% of effort. *Find
  the channels/customers/content driving most results; cut the rest.*
- **Local vs. Global Optima** — Don't perfect the wrong thing. *Optimizing
  subject lines won't help if email is the wrong channel.*
- **Theory of Constraints** — One bottleneck limits throughput; fix it first.
  *If the funnel converts but traffic is low, fix traffic before CRO.*
- **Opportunity Cost** — Every choice forfeits alternatives. *Time on a low-ROI
  channel is time off high-ROI activity.*
- **Law of Diminishing Returns** — Added investment yields smaller gains past a
  point. *The 10th blog post rarely matches the first; know when to diversify.*
- **Second-Order Thinking** — Consider effects of effects. *A flash sale lifts
  revenue (1st order) but can train customers to wait for discounts (2nd).*
- **Map ≠ Territory** — Models/data aren't reality. *Your persona is useful but
  real customers are messier; stay in touch with actual users.*
- **Probabilistic Thinking** — Think in likelihoods, plan for multiple outcomes.
  *Don't bet everything on one campaign; spread risk.*
- **Barbell Strategy** — Combine extreme safety with small high-risk bets; avoid
  the mediocre middle. *~80% proven channels, ~20% experiments.*

## 2. Understanding Buyers & Human Psychology

How customers think, decide, and behave.

- **Fundamental Attribution Error** — We blame others' character over their
  situation. *When customers don't convert, examine your process first — the
  cause is usually situational (confusing checkout), not personal.*
- **Mere Exposure Effect** — Familiarity breeds liking. *Consistent multi-channel
  presence builds preference over time.*
- **Availability Heuristic** — People judge likelihood by what comes to mind.
  *Case studies and testimonials make success feel achievable.*
- **Confirmation Bias** — People seek confirming info. *Align messaging with what
  the audience already believes; fighting beliefs head-on rarely works.*
- **Lindy Effect** — The longer something survives, the longer it likely lasts.
  *Proven fundamentals (clear value props, social proof) outlast fads.*
- **Mimetic Desire** — People want what others want. *Waitlists, exclusivity, and
  social proof trigger contagious desire.*
- **Sunk Cost Fallacy** — Past investment irrationally justifies continuing.
  *Kill underperforming campaigns; past spend doesn't justify future spend.*
- **Endowment Effect** — People value what they own more. *Free trials, samples,
  freemium create ownership and reluctance to give it up.*
- **IKEA Effect** — People value what they helped build. *Let customers
  customize/configure; effort raises perceived value and commitment.*
- **Zero-Price Effect** — "Free" is psychologically distinct, not just cheap.
  *Free tiers/trials/shipping have outsized appeal; $1→$0 beats $2→$1.*
- **Hyperbolic Discounting / Present Bias** — Immediate rewards win over future
  ones. *Emphasize "start saving time today" over "ROI in 6 months."*
- **Status-Quo Bias** — People prefer the current state. *Reduce switching
  friction: "Import your data in one click."*
- **Default Effect** — People accept pre-selected options. *Pre-select the plan
  you want chosen (ethically); opt-out beats opt-in.*
- **Paradox of Choice** — Too many options paralyze. *Three tiers beat seven;
  recommend a single "best for most."*
- **Goal-Gradient Effect** — Effort accelerates near a goal. *Progress bars,
  completion %, "almost there" messaging drive completion.*
- **Peak-End Rule** — Experiences are judged by peak and end, not average.
  *Design memorable peaks (surprise upgrades) and strong endings (thank-you /
  follow-up).*
- **Zeigarnik Effect** — Unfinished tasks nag the mind. *"You're 80% done,"
  abandoned carts, incomplete profiles create pull to finish.*
- **Pratfall Effect** — A small flaw makes the competent more likable. *"We're
  not the cheapest, but…" can increase trust and differentiation.*
- **Curse of Knowledge** — Experts can't imagine not knowing. *Your product seems
  obvious to you, confusing to newcomers; test copy with outsiders.*
- **Mental Accounting** — Money is treated differently by source/use. *"$3/day"
  feels different from "$90/month" though identical.*
- **Regret Aversion** — People avoid actions that might cause regret. *Money-back
  guarantees, free trials, "no commitment" reduce regret fear.*
- **Bandwagon Effect / Social Proof** — People follow the crowd. *Customer
  counts, testimonials, logos, reviews, "trending" build confidence.*

## 3. Influencing Behavior & Persuasion

Ethical levers for customer decisions (Cialdini-rooted plus framing).

- **Reciprocity** — People repay favors. *Free content/tools/generous tiers
  create reciprocal obligation; give value before asking.*
- **Commitment & Consistency** — Small commitments lead to larger ones. *Get an
  email signup or free trial first; the next step gets easier.*
- **Authority Bias** — People defer to credible experts. *Expert endorsements,
  certifications, "featured in," thought leadership.*
- **Liking / Similarity Bias** — People say yes to those they like and resemble.
  *Relatable spokespeople, founder stories: "by marketers, for marketers."*
- **Unity Principle** — Shared identity drives influence. *Position the brand as
  part of the customer's tribe; insider language and shared values.*
- **Scarcity / Urgency** — Limited availability raises perceived value. *Limited
  beta spots, real deadlines, low-stock warnings — only when genuine.*
- **Foot-in-the-Door** — Small request first, then escalate. *Free trial → paid →
  annual → enterprise.*
- **Door-in-the-Face** — Large request first, then retreat to the real ask. *Show
  enterprise pricing first; the starter plan then feels reasonable.*
- **Loss Aversion / Prospect Theory** — Losses hurt ~2x equivalent gains. *Frame
  what they lose by not acting; "don't miss out" beats "you could gain."*
- **Anchoring** — The first number dominates later judgments. *Show the higher
  price first (original / competitor / enterprise tier).*
- **Decoy Effect** — A third inferior option makes another look better. *A clearly
  worse tier makes your target tier the obvious choice.*
- **Framing Effect** — Presentation changes perception. *"90% success" vs "10%
  failure" — same fact, frame positively.*
- **Contrast Effect** — Things are judged against comparisons. *Show the "before"
  clearly so the "after" looks vivid.*

> Ethics note: use scarcity, defaults, and urgency only on real constraints.
> Manufactured urgency (fake countdowns, fake stock counts) erodes trust and
> can be deceptive. See the EAST/ethical guardrails in SKILL.md.

## 4. Pricing Psychology

How people perceive and respond to prices.

- **Charm Pricing / Left-Digit Effect** — Prices ending in 9 read much lower.
  *$99 feels far cheaper than $100; use .99/.95 for value products.*
- **Rounded-Price (Fluency) Effect** — Round numbers feel premium and process
  easily. *$500/month signals quality; $497 signals value.*
- **Rule of 100** — Under $100, percentage discounts look bigger; over $100,
  absolute discounts look bigger. *$80: "20% off" > "$16 off." $500: "$100 off"
  > "20% off."*
- **Price Relativity / Good-Better-Best** — Prices are judged relative to options
  shown. *Three tiers with the middle as target; expensive tier makes it look
  reasonable, cheap tier anchors.*
- **Mental Accounting (Pricing)** — Reframing the same price changes perception.
  *"$1/day" < "$30/month"; "less than your morning coffee."*

## 5. Design & Delivery Models

Designing effective marketing systems and behavior flows.

- **Hick's Law** — Decision time rises with options. *One clear CTA beats three;
  fewer form fields beat more.*
- **AIDA Funnel** — Attention → Interest → Desire → Action. *Structure pages to
  move through each stage; capture attention before building desire.*
- **Rule of 7** — ~7 touchpoints before converting. *Multi-touch campaigns:
  retargeting, email sequences, consistent presence compound.*
- **Nudge Theory / Choice Architecture** — Small presentation changes shift
  decisions. *Default selections, strategic ordering, friction reduction.*
- **BJ Fogg Behavior Model** — Behavior = Motivation × Ability × Prompt; all
  three needed. *High motivation + hard task = no action; design for all three.*
- **EAST Framework** — Make behaviors Easy, Attractive, Social, Timely. *Reduce
  friction, make appealing, show others doing it, ask at the right moment.*
- **COM-B Model** — Behavior needs Capability, Opportunity, Motivation. *Can they
  do it? Is the path clear? Do they want to? Address all three.*
- **Activation Energy** — Initial energy to start blocks action even when the
  task is easy. *Pre-fill forms, offer templates, show quick wins; make the
  first step trivial.*
- **North Star Metric** — One metric capturing delivered value; focus aligns
  effort. *Pick it (active users, completed projects, revenue/customer) and
  align everything.*
- **Cobra Effect** — Incentives backfire into opposite results. *Test incentive
  structures; a referral bonus may attract low-quality gaming.*

## 6. Growth & Scaling Models

How marketing compounds and scales.

- **Feedback Loops** — Output becomes input. *Build virtuous cycles: more users →
  more content → better SEO → more users.*
- **Compounding** — Small consistent gains accumulate; early gains matter most.
  *Consistent content/SEO/brand compound; start early.*
- **Network Effects** — Value rises with more users. *Shared workspaces,
  integrations, marketplaces, communities.*
- **Flywheel Effect** — Sustained effort builds self-maintaining momentum.
  *Content → traffic → leads → customers → case studies → more content.*
- **Switching Costs** — Cost of leaving creates retention. *Integrations, data
  accumulation, workflow customization, team adoption — raise ethically.*
- **Exploration vs. Exploitation** — Balance new bets against optimizing what
  works. *Don't abandon working channels; reserve budget for experiments.*
- **Critical Mass / Tipping Point** — Threshold past which growth self-sustains.
  *Reach critical mass in one segment before expanding — depth before breadth.*
- **Survivorship Bias** — Focusing on visible successes, ignoring hidden
  failures. *Study failed campaigns; the viral hit had 99 invisible failures.*
