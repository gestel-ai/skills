# Launch Playbook (Reference)

Deep reference for `gestel-launch`. Use it to plan, draft, and review launch
work. It contains no live platform claims — treat any platform mechanics below
as general principles that must be re-verified against current docs before you
state them as fact.

## ORB Channel Framework

Structure launch marketing across three channel types. Everything should
ultimately funnel back to owned channels.

### Owned channels

You control the channel (not the audience): email list, blog, podcast, branded
community (Slack/Discord), website/product.

- Compound over time; no algorithm or pay-to-play volatility; direct
  relationship with the audience.
- Start with 1-2 based on the audience: weak industry content → blog; people
  want direct updates → email; engagement matters → community.
- Pattern (Superhuman): invite-only waitlist + one-on-one onboarding demos
  created exclusivity, FOMO, and word-of-mouth through owned relationships.

### Rented channels

Visibility you don't control: social (X, LinkedIn, Instagram), app stores and
marketplaces, YouTube, Reddit.

- Pick 1-2 platforms where the audience is active; use them to drive traffic to
  owned channels; don't rely on them as the only strategy.
- Tactics: X threads → newsletter; LinkedIn value posts → gated content/email;
  marketplace listings (Shopify, Slack) → optimize, then drive to site.
- Pattern (Notion): viral templates/workflows on X, YouTube, Reddit, all funneled
  into signups + email onboarding.

### Borrowed channels

Tap someone else's audience: guest content, collaborations (webinars,
co-marketing), speaking, influencer partnerships.

- Be proactive: list leaders the audience follows, pitch win-win collabs, find
  audience overlap, set up affiliate/referral incentives.
- Pattern (TRMNL): a free unit sent to a YouTuber (no paid sponsorship) produced
  a 500K+ view review and large sales, plus an affiliate program for ongoing
  promotion.
- Borrowed attention only pays off when converted into owned relationships.

## Five-Phase Launch Approach

Launching is a phased process that builds momentum, not a single day.

1. **Internal launch** — recruit early users one-on-one, collect usability/feature
   feedback, ensure a demo-able prototype. Goal: validate core functionality.
2. **Alpha launch** — landing page with early-access form, announce existence,
   invite users individually, MVP working in production. Goal: first external
   validation + waitlist seed.
3. **Beta launch** — work the early-access list (some free, some paid), tease the
   problems you solve, recruit friends/investors/influencers. Add: coming-soon
   page, "Beta" sticker, email invites, early-access toggle. Goal: buzz + broader
   feedback.
4. **Early access launch** — leak screenshots/GIFs/demos, gather quant usage +
   qual feedback, run user research (incentivize with credits), optional PMF
   survey. Expand by throttled batches (5-10%) or all-at-once "early access".
   Goal: validate at scale.
5. **Full launch** — open self-serve signups, start charging, announce GA across
   all channels. Touchpoints: customer emails, in-app popups/tours, website
   banner, "New" sticker, blog post, social, and submission targets like Product
   Hunt / BetaList / Hacker News. Goal: maximum visibility + conversion.

## Product Hunt Launch Strategy

Powerful for early adopters but not magic — it requires preparation.

- **Pros:** tech-savvy early-adopter exposure, credibility bump (esp. Product of
  the Day), potential PR/backlinks.
- **Cons:** very competitive, short-lived traffic spike, heavy pre-launch prep.
- **Before launch day:** build relationships with supporters and communities;
  optimize the listing (tagline, visuals, short demo video); study successful
  launches; provide value in communities before pitching; prep the team for
  all-day engagement.
- **On launch day:** treat it as an all-day event; respond to every comment in
  real time; spark discussion; rally your existing audience; direct traffic to
  your site to capture signups.
- **After launch day:** follow up with everyone who engaged; convert traffic into
  owned relationships (email); keep momentum with post-launch content.
- **Patterns:** SavvyCal (#2 Product of the Month) — pre-optimized landing/
  onboarding, pre-built influencer relationships, replied to every comment.
  Reform (#1 Product of the Day) — studied prior launches, clear tagline + demo
  video, value-first community engagement, all-day event, traffic → signups.

## Post-Launch Product Marketing

The launch isn't over at announcement — adoption and retention work begins.

- Educate new users with an automated onboarding email sequence.
- Reinforce the launch in the recurring roundup email to catch people who missed it.
- Differentiate with comparison pages.
- Update web pages with dedicated sections on the new feature/product.
- Offer a hands-on interactive demo so visitors can explore before signing up.
- It is easier to build on existing momentum than to start from scratch.

## Ongoing Launch Cadence

Don't rely on one event. Prioritize each update by impact:

- **Major** (new features, overhauls) → full campaign across channels (blog,
  email, in-app, social).
- **Medium** (integrations, UI enhancements) → targeted email to relevant
  segments + in-app banner.
- **Minor** (bug fixes, tweaks) → changelog / release notes that signal progress.

Tactics: space out releases, reuse high-performing announcement formats, keep
engaging via email/social/in-app, and signal active development (even small
changelog updates build retention and word-of-mouth).

## Launch Checklist

**Pre-launch:** landing page with clear value prop; email/waitlist capture;
early-access list built; owned channels established; rented channel profiles
optimized; borrowed-channel opportunities identified; Product Hunt listing
prepared (if using); launch assets created (screenshots, demo video, GIFs);
onboarding flow ready; analytics/tracking in place.

**Launch day:** announcement email; blog post published; social posts posted;
Product Hunt live (if using); in-app announcement; website banner active; team
ready to engage; monitoring for issues/feedback.

**Post-launch:** onboarding email sequence active; follow up with engaged
prospects; roundup email includes the announcement; comparison pages published;
interactive demo created; gather and act on feedback; plan the next launch moment.

## Discovery Questions

1. What are you launching (new product, major feature, minor update)?
2. Current audience size and engagement?
3. What owned channels exist (email list size, blog traffic, community)?
4. Timeline for launch?
5. Have you launched before — what worked / didn't?
6. Considering Product Hunt? Preparation status?
